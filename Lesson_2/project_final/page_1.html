Existentialist Thinkers

Friedrich Nietzsche

Friedrich Nietzsche was a German philosopher who was born on October 15, 1844 in the small town of Röcken bei Lützen, Prussia. He was the oldest child of Carl Ludwig Nietzsche, a Lutheran pastor, and Franziska Nietzsche, a homemaker. Nietzsche was a brilliant student who excelled in classical literature, music, and theology. In 1864, Nietzsche enrolled at the University of Bonn to study theology and classical philology. However, he quickly became disillusioned with the study of theology and turned his attention to philosophy, especially the works of Arthur Schopenhauer and Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling. In 1865, Nietzsche transferred to the University of Leipzig, where he studied under the renowned philologist Friedrich Ritschl. After completing his studies, Nietzsche worked as a professor of classical philology at the University of Basel in Switzerland. He quickly gained a reputation as a brilliant and original thinker, and his first book, The Birth of Tragedy, was published in 1872. In this book, Nietzsche rejected the traditional view of art as a means of representation and instead argued that art was a means of creating meaning and overcoming nihilism. Throughout his career, Nietzsche developed a number of influential ideas, including the concept of the "will to power," the idea that individuals are driven by a fundamental desire to exert their power and control over their environment. He also rejected traditional moral values, arguing that they were arbitrary and did not reflect the true nature of the world. Instead, he believed that individuals should seek to live in a state of "self-overcoming," constantly striving to become stronger and more powerful. Nietzsche's later works, including Thus Spoke Zarathustra and Beyond Good and Evil, further developed these ideas and helped to establish him as one of the most important and controversial philosophers of the 19th century. However, Nietzsche's health began to decline in the late 1880s, and he eventually suffered a mental breakdown in 1889. He spent the remaining years of his life in a state of mental illness, and he died on August 25, 1900 at the age of 55. Despite his early death, Nietzsche's ideas continue to influence philosophy, psychology, and popular culture to this day.

Jean-Paul Sartre

Jean-Paul Sartre was a French philosopher and writer who is widely regarded as one of the key figures in the philosophy of existentialism. Born in 1905 in Paris, Sartre came from a middle-class family and showed an early aptitude for academics, excelling in literature and philosophy in high school. After earning his degree in philosophy from the École Normale Supérieure, Sartre began teaching the subject at a number of institutions, including the prestigious Lycée Henri IV. It was during this time that he became interested in the work of German philosopher Martin Heidegger, who would have a profound influence on Sartre's thinking. Sartre's philosophical views were strongly influenced by Heidegger's concept of "being-in-the-world," which emphasizes the individual's relationship to the world and their role in shaping their own existence. This led Sartre to develop his famous notion that "existence precedes essence," which asserts that individuals must create their own meaning and purpose in life, rather than seeking it in pre-existing ideas or beliefs. In addition to his philosophical work, Sartre was also a prominent writer and political activist. He wrote several novels, plays, and essays, including the widely-acclaimed "Being and Nothingness" and "No Exit." He was also involved in left-wing politics, and was a vocal critic of colonialism and imperialism. Sartre's ideas and writings had a major impact on 20th-century philosophy and literature, and he is still widely studied and discussed today. He died in 1980, leaving behind a rich intellectual legacy that continues to inspire and challenge readers around the world.

Søren Kierkegaard

Søren Kierkegaard was a Danish philosopher and theologian who is widely considered to be the father of existentialism. Born in 1813 in Copenhagen, Kierkegaard grew up in a wealthy family and was educated in the finest schools. He studied philosophy, theology, and literature, and was heavily influenced by the works of Plato, Aristotle, and Immanuel Kant. Kierkegaard's early writings focused on the nature of faith and religion, and he was particularly interested in exploring the concept of subjective truth. He believed that true meaning and fulfillment could only be found by individuals through their own personal experiences and choices, rather than through societal norms or institutions. Kierkegaard's most famous works include "Either/Or," "The Sickness Unto Death," and "Fear and Trembling." These works emphasized the importance of subjective experience and individual choice in the search for meaning in a seemingly meaningless world. In his later years, Kierkegaard became increasingly critical of the church and organized religion, and he often used his writings to challenge conventional ideas about faith and morality. He died in 1855 at the age of 42, but his ideas continue to influence philosophy and theology to this day.
